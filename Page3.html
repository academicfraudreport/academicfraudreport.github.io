Read the following three essays (Awaywithwords is fairly short and Tufte's powerpoint critique has plenty of pictures)

    "The Politics of the Interface"
    "awaywithwords"
    "Tuft PowerPoint"

    Each of these essays takes a different stab at how technology reifies, norms, legitimizes, and exercises its domain over users. What competing ideas are present in these essays about the agentive power of technology? Do you get the sense that technology critiques lean too hard toward technology determinism? Or is there a more fine line?

    Reading Response 2

    To answer the questions, here are my two main points first followed by a more detailed analysis:

    (1) Technology does not dictate society’s nature and culture. People can choose to engage with technology or not. Engaging with technology is not compulsory (unless we’re talking about that big, fat violation of the Nuremburg Code, vaccine mandates, that I ain’t ever engaging; and what a coincidence that one of the essays mentions NASA, one of the other results of WWII "Operation Paperclip." Way to bring it all together, Prof.).

    (2) "Agentive power" is another way of saying "proxy." And another way to say "proxy" is "astroturfing." These three essays all blame technology for disseminating and perpetuating white male hegemony, colonialism, and capitalism to the detriment of women and nonwhites. But here’s the thing... in this country, people have the opportunity to go out and create their own businesses and generate their own prosperity and nobody can stop them except them. There are all kinds of grants out there for women and nonwhites to do exactly that if they feel like the corporate arena is stifling their aptitudes. But they need to have something to offer and they’d better not allow certain political factions to psychologically condition them to undermine themselves through shame and victimhood or they will fail in their ventures and in life. Never engage in self-deception.

    I’ll start with the essay, "awaywithwords." This essay doesn’t work in the context of corporate content management systems. The author comes off as someone who has romanticized the role of technical writers to the point of being totally out of touch with the reality of technical writing in the corporate arena [currently being outsourced and offshored]. This essay is dated and loaded with pseudo intellectualism masquerading as "truth."

    The essay entitled, "The Cognitive Style of PowerPoint," brings up a good point about the space shuttle Columbia disaster. Engineers made the mistake of trying to condense complex information into summarized bullet points without distributing accompanying reports that fully explained the danger of explosion upon reentry into Earth’s atmosphere and its effect on the seven lives aboard the shuttle. But then the author goes on to blame Microsoft and corporate culture for imposing PowerPoint on the populous. Using any Microsoft product is a choice and people are responsible for the consequences of their choices, not the corporation selling the software. Personally, I’ve worked in both the private and public sectors and I have never seen any scientist or CPA disseminate complex information in PowerPoint. They write long reports and distribute those reports to board members. Besides that, PowerPoint is excellent for Human Resources orientation presentations. Also, the I.T. Dept. comes in and disseminates computer use rules via PowerPoint presentations so it works there too. That’s pretty simple information that is perfectly suited to presentation software.

    Now for the essay entitled, "The Politics of the Interface." This essay in particular incorporates every tenet of critical race theory including intersectionality and Marxism. This essay engages in the hypocrisy logical fallacy when it seeks to use "anti-racism" to try to undermine others via racism and sexism (target white men exclusively) [while claiming that those who created the technology that made this article and its dissemination possible are motivated by racism.] It’s also hypocritical for English teachers, professors, and other political factions to use youth as their personal "proxies" in order to undermine society while hiding out in their artificially manufactured leftist fiefdoms feeding at the taxpayer trough. Action without consequence does not exist. And no, critical race theory is not "widely accepted pedagogy" if 23 states have banned it or are in the process of banning it.

    Besides the aforementioned essay being steeped in CRT, which is a circular argument logical fallacy [a circular argument assumes what it attempts to prove], the authors engage in a false equivalence logical fallacy when they compare the computer science industry to the maintaining of border security. These are two dissimilar things. Further, the authors engage in the exaggeration logical fallacy and hypocrisy logical fallacy [again] when they state that:

    Students who want to use computers are continually confronted with these grand narratives which foreground a value on middleclass, corporate culture; capitalism and the commodification of information; Standard English; and rationalistic ways of representing knowledge. These values simultaneously do violence to and encourage the rejection of the languages of different races and the values of non-dominant cultural and gender groups. When students from these groups enter the linguistic borderlands of the interface, in other words, they often learn that they must abandon their own culture or gender and acknowledge the dominance of other groups.

    Firstly, there’s no ethnic cleansing going on in this country that forces students who use computers to abandon their own cultures, languages, and genders. That’s an exaggeration. Students can participate in computer literacy and economic viability and still retain their identities. It’s a choice. Secondly, the English teachers who wrote this essay seeking to impose their leftist ideology on students are themselves middle class and trying to exert [their own brand of] "dominance." That’s hypocritical [while accusing computer science proprietors of hypocrisy]. Most of these people go directly from being a college student to being a teacher or professor. That means they have zero experience working in the private sector, never served in the military, never lived abroad (especially not in hostile territory), and never actually had to fight for their own survival because they grew up in "quiet, peaceful neighborhoods." Experience is the source of wisdom; books are the source of knowledge. Wisdom and knowledge are very different things.

    That said, all three of these essays reek of envy and bitterness towards successful men in particular and that’s disturbing, especially since no counter viewpoints are offered in the reading. Personally, I like successful men and I especially like strong, successful men. As a woman (not chest-feeding person) I don’t feel like a victim and have zero interest in undermining men because I am secure in my own aptitudes and person, and have a lot of experience navigating predators.

    The truth is that technical writers have been, or are being, phased out of the corporate arena via outsourcing and offshoring of their jobs. The market does determine business decisions of this nature to a large degree, but this is being done with the skill and proficiency of corporate attorneys deliberately moving these [woke] people out of corporations. My perspective on that is that corporate leaders don’t appreciate those without business and computer science expertise coming in and telling them how to run their businesses while not bringing much to the table outside of [woke] ideology. But I would really like to know what Thomas Sowell thinks of this situation from an economic, conservative perspective.

    PROFESSOR COMMENTS

    I see you rejecting the arguments of each of these readings at face value. For what it’s worth, the reading responses serve as a tool to allow students to analyze common arguments and critiques about technology. Your analysis, however, remains entirely on the surface. There are any number of ways to critique and analyze what is presented here while still disagreeing with the implications and arguments of each. In future reading responses do try to engage with what is being said with a clear and analytical approach. Humor and style are welcome additions but are not substitutions for strength of analysis. Half Credit.
